Framing the
Invisible

The practice of liubai, or intentionally leaving white space in Song Dynasty paintings, not only reflects the aesthetics of that era, but also provides insight into a way of life that remains relevant to people even today.

One of the key discernible characteristics of a classical Chinese painting is in its concise style and composition, particularly with much of the canvas intentionally left blank—an element also known as liubai. While Western art similarly possesses the concept of negative space, which deals mainly with spatial hierarchy, this intended blankness in Chinese paintings was a way for artists to express their philosophical and aesthetic beliefs, and to explore the relationship between their inner world and the natural environment.

During the Song, ink and white paper became increasingly important artistic mediums. “As both mediums became more widely used, the relationship between black and white, and blankness and solidness, also became more prominent,” explains Professor Huang Xiaofeng from the Central Academy of Fine Arts.

“Liubai is not only a way to express emptiness, but also an expression of reality.”

With liubai, artists were able to convey their intentions beyond ink applied to paper, and beyond the sum of the painting’s parts. It was through leaving space—using less to achieve more—that they could craft dramatic perspectives, movement, and balance.

Beauty in restraint

As artists’ techniques matured throughout the Southern Song, they manipulated blank spaces to convey the key elements of a landscape, such as the seemingly endless sky or a body of water.

The emergence of the ‘one-corner’ or ‘half-side’ composition style helped to capture the viewer’s imagination. An Angler Going Home is representative of this style, with the rocky precipice and a tree occupying the top right corner of the painting. Directly beyond the tree, a fisherman walks along the coast in the distance, whilst the bottom left corner is left blank to imply the expansive ocean and emphasize the scene’s tranquility.

Featuring a similar asymmetrical composition, Pine Tree and Cottage by Southern Song court-appointed artist Yan Ciping uses a precise ‘axe-cut’ technique to breathe life and texture into a field of solids, framing the villa and trees as the painting’s main subject. Depicted alongside an expansive void conveying water and sky, the feeling conveyed is one of scale and tremendous distance, implied to stretch beyond the frame of the fan painting.

A MOVING PERSPECTIVE

Liubai was also used by Song artists to instill a dynamic experience of a first-person journey as the viewer ‘traveled’ through landscape paintings.

“An important function of character setting in landscape painting was to arouse the viewer's empathy and make them feel immersed in the scene.”

During the Northern Song Dynasty, he explains, father and son scholar-artists Guo Xi and Guo Si compiled a landscape painting theory titled ‘Lofty Accomplishment Among Forest and Streams’ that describes a landscape painting as one that should give the viewer a sense of “feasibility, viewability, travelability, and livability.”

In Guo Xi’s most famous painting, Early Spring , he left blanks between the rocky peaks to represent the fog and mist partially obscuring them, guiding the viewer’s eye to observe nature as if they are moving through the painting. The distant mountains gradually fade to white, creating a strong sense of height, depth, and space, while also giving balance to the composition. By using liubai, Guo deftly manipulates space and time, creating what is considered one of the pinnacles of Chinese landscape art.

Fan Kuan’s Travelers Among Mountains and Streams is also exemplary of liubai to build distance and create a sense of journey, with the midground intentionally left blank to depict mist, and the addition of minuscule human figures along the winding road placing the monumentality of the landscape in full perspective.

THE BALANCE OF NATURE

At a time of unprecedented knowledge exchange, the prevailing philosophy during the Song called for a return to basic Confucian teachings, which valued balance and harmony between humans and nature.

“Artists paid more attention to natural weather events as well as on the detailed expression of various [human] characters that emphasized how human activity was closely connected with mountains, rivers, landforms and natural phenomena.”

“As the study of geography and geology developed during the Song, so did landscape painting,” says Professor Huang.

More than creating interesting spatial hierarchy, depth, and movement, liubai also became a way to instill peaceful balance between man and nature in painted landscapes. This is reflected in Southern Song painter Liang Kai’s Landscape in Snow in which liubai is used to emphasize the depth of space and create an arresting atmosphere of the wintery landscape.

One of the masterpieces attributed to legendary Southern Song artist Ma Yuan, Solitary Angler on a Wintry River depicts a lone fisherman who is still fishing in the river despite the apparent bitter cold. The vast field of blankness dominating the picture highlights the fisherman’s isolation from the world and his unaffected demeanor—suggesting that he is calmly persisting and enjoying the moment—and illustrates the cultural virtues of peaceful stillness.

Scholar Reclining and Watching Rising Clouds , painted by Ma Yuan’s son Ma Lin, is a direct illustration of a renowned poem by literati Wang Wei which includes the lines: “I walk unto where the waters end… and sit down to watch when clouds arise,” and is a celebrated example of the integration of poetry and painting. Much of the painting is left blank to depict a barely visible mountain in the distance, with the resulting calm reflecting the poem’s message of hope and virtuousness. An imperial gift featuring the emperor’s calligraphy, this painting serves as a reminder on how to keep a clear heart that is detached from gains and losses—a message relevant to us even today.

“The concept of liubai continues to be reinterpreted today, seen in many aspects of life, art, and philosophy specific to Eastern aesthetics.”

“It can help us better appreciate and understand ancient paintings, and in turn, add to our understanding of the world today.”

From tracing vernacular stories to documenting key moments in court life; daily whimsies to the most sublime of philosophical thoughts, the breadth of Song-era paintings in today’s collections reveal much about this early sophisticated Chinese society’s wealth of appreciation for the natural world around them.
